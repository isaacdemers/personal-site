I have applied to Chick-fil-A's Leadership Development Program three times.

The first two times I was told no. The third time, in March 2025, I was told yes. I have learned that some dreams do not die when you are rejected. They just wait for a timeline that is not yours.

My story did not start at Chick-fil-A. It started in Toronto, where my parents built a life around serving people most of the world had written off. It moved to Newport News at eleven, where I sat alone in a youth group and learned what it feels like to be unseen. It found its footing in Cumberland, Maryland at thirteen, where a small Appalachian church gave me faith, belonging, and the friendships I still carry. Every move stripped something away and replaced it with conviction.

I spent six years at Rocky Gap Casino and Resort climbing from valet attendant to Hotel Operations Supervisor and Marketing Manager. I left a career I had built to start over as a kitchen team member at Chick-fil-A Ridgeside Drive in Mount Airy. Three years later I had helped build a top-ranked drive-thru in Maryland and developed leaders who now run that operation, including one who was just accepted into LDP himself. Then I got the yes I had been waiting on for years.

This past year has been the hardest and most rewarding chapter of my life. Louisiana tested whether I could turn numbers. Fort Worth tested whether I could hold a team together through power outages, sewage backups, a leaking ceiling, and a confidential operator transition in the same five months. I am now in Albany, New York serving as a Grand Opening Supervisor, learning what it takes to launch a restaurant from nothing. Every assignment has been preparation for the same calling I have been chasing my whole life. To become a Chick-fil-A Owner-Operator, and to stir dreams in the people I lead the way my father stirred them in me.

The Roots

I come from people who built things with their hands and their hearts.

My father's parents, Henry and Pauline Demers, ran a business in New Hampshire where every client felt heard. My grandmother was a nurse. My grandfather was a Korean War veteran with an entrepreneur's instinct. On my mother's side, my grandfather David Hawbaker was a stonemason who worked with his hands his entire life. My grandmother Nancy kept a home where you became family the moment you walked through the door. It did not matter your history, your race, or your religion. You were in.

My parents met at a small theology school in Charleston, Maine. My mother grew up in Clear Spring, Maryland. My father grew up between Manchester and Bedford, New Hampshire. They fell in love, moved to Toronto, and built a life around serving people most of the world had given up on.

Toronto

My brother Levi and I were raised near downtown Toronto in one of the most diverse cities in the world.

My father worked as an addictions counselor with the Salvation Army. Our dinner table was full of people from every walk of life, and he had a way of seeing past the addiction and the mistakes to the person underneath. To who they were trying to become. Every Sunday we loaded vans of food and equipment and drove to a community center to serve hot lunches. Every Christmas we rang bells for the Christmas Kettle program.

While other kids followed the Lego instructions, I threw them away and built hotels and businesses. When I was eight, I spent every dollar of my allowance on lemonade and set up a stand outside our apartment building during the summer garage sale. I did not find as much success as I hoped, but I fell in love with the idea that you can create something out of nothing. That feeling never left me. My parents enrolled me in a program called Discover Ability, where I found art and creativity and never let them go.

Newport News and Cumberland

I was eleven the night before we moved to Newport News, Virginia. I remember lying on the floor of our empty twelfth-story apartment, staring at the ceiling, realizing I was leaving everything I knew behind.

We moved into a small apartment in Monarch Crossing. We did not have a car. Walking two and a half miles to Walmart became routine. Our church was an all-Black congregation and my family was the only white family there. There were Sundays I sat alone in youth group. It taught me what it feels like to be unseen, and it made me decide, even then, that no one I ever led would feel that way.

It was also in Newport News that I walked into a Chick-fil-A for the first time. My uncle had told our family about it and we went in with high expectations. I remember watching a team member walk through the dining room offering refills and mints. That level of care in a fast-food restaurant stopped me. I had never seen anything like it outside of a fine-dining setting or a five-star resort. I did not know it at the time, but that moment made me a fan for life.

Two years later we moved to Cumberland, Maryland for my father's chaplaincy at a maximum-security prison. Cumberland is small. Eighteen thousand people tucked into the Appalachian foothills. It is also where I found Central Assembly of God, the church that gave me the first sense of belonging I had felt outside my own family. My faith became my own there. I attended Calvary Christian Academy, played basketball, competed in national Fine Arts in photography and videography, won the Voice of Democracy writing competition, and graduated with honors. Then I worked full-time and earned a Bachelor of Science from Liberty University Cum Laude.

Every chapter stripped something away. Every chapter replaced it with conviction.

Rocky Gap

At eighteen I parked cars at Rocky Gap Casino and Resort. I stayed for six years and rose through five roles to Hotel Operations Supervisor and Marketing Manager.

I built systems. I built playbooks. But mostly, I built people. Josh James grew into a Hotel Operations Supervisor who led his team through a complex COVID reopening. Angelina Bloom became the strongest supervisor I had. Jacob Santameyer and Brandon Stotler grew into supervisors of their own. I did not just work at Rocky Gap for six years. I built people there, and that is the part I am still proud of.

Then COVID shut the world down. I was unemployed for three months. When I came back, it was behind a desk as Marketing Manager. I missed serving people face-to-face. I missed the conversations, the smiles, the guest interaction that made me fall in love with hospitality in the first place. That disconnect, combined with a corporate culture that treated every guest as a dollar sign and not a relationship, pushed me toward the next chapter.

My brother Levi, a former Chick-fil-A Training Director, kept telling me about a brand whose values matched the way I already wanted to lead. Before my first LDP interview, I spent a full day shadowing Jason Bearinger, a local Chick-fil-A Operator. In eight hours I watched a man own every corner of his business, the freedom, the weight, the impact he had on his team and his community. I knew that day I wanted to build the same thing. I applied externally for LDP. I was told no.

The Pivot

I left a six-year career and started over as a kitchen team member at Chick-fil-A Ridgeside Drive in Mount Airy, Maryland.

Ridgeside became my home for over three years. I built our Drive-Thru Playbook from the ground up. We climbed to the top of the Maryland drive-thru rankings. We broke throughput records. But the part that matters most to me is the people. Danny Caporotti, Harry Horyat, and Izzy Reyes all grew into director roles under my mentorship. Danny was just accepted into LDP himself. To play any part in someone else's journey to this program, knowing what it has meant in my own life, is something I will never take lightly.

While I was building at Ridgeside I applied to LDP a second time. I made it to the third round. I was told no again.

I applied a third time. In March 2025, I was told yes. Some dreams do not die. They just take longer than you planned.

LDP

LDP has been the hardest and most rewarding chapter of my life.

Mall of Louisiana tested whether I could turn numbers in a difficult environment. I promoted directors and moved the metrics. Altamesa and McCart in Fort Worth tested something different. Whether I could hold a team together through chaos. Power outages, sewage backups, a leaking ceiling, and a confidential operator transition all in the same five months. I held the team together, promoted directors there too, and was told I was performing ahead of expectations.

I am now in Albany, New York, serving as a Grand Opening Supervisor and learning what it takes to launch a restaurant from nothing. Every assignment has been preparation for the same calling.

"Performing ahead of expectations. His relational leadership is transformational."

Walt Anderson, LDP Transition Lead

Philosophy

S. Truett Cathy — the philosophy I inherited

S. Truett Cathy
1921 to 2014, Founder of Chick-fil-A

Truett Cathy opened the first Dwarf Grill in 1946 in Hapeville, Georgia. He built Chick-fil-A on the belief that a business exists not only to serve customers, but to develop people. He was still at work into his eighties. The business was never the point. The people were.

Two of his convictions shape how I think about operating a restaurant. The first: it is better to build boys than mend men. The second: every person has worth beyond what they produce. Both of those ideas live in my Dream Fund — a dedicated line item in my operating model that funds team member education, growth, and community investment from day one.

"It is better to build boys than mend men."

The Dream Fund is built into my operation from day one. It funds team member growth, education, and community investment. I am not waiting for the restaurant to be profitable to begin. The fund is the business model.

"We should be about more than just selling chicken."

That conviction is the reason my return to Manchester is not about market share. It is about extending a legacy that was already being built there long before I applied to LDP.

Greater Manchester is home to more than 400,000 people and has three Chick-fil-A locations. The market opportunity is real. But the reason I want to be there runs deeper than market share. My grandfather built there. My family is still there. I am not entering that community. I am returning to it.

First baptism, small creek in Maryland, age 10

My father baptized me in a small creek in Maryland near my grandparents' home. Just immediate family. I was ten, and for me that first baptism was a symbol that my faith had become mine. I was taking ownership. I did not believe in Christ because my parents told me to. I believed in Christ because of what he did in my life.

Formation

Fine Arts competition, Calvary Christian Academy

I competed in national Fine Arts competitions in photography and videography, and I carry a camera with me to this day. Creativity is not a hobby for me. It is how I pay attention to people and places.

I had not given God the keys to my destiny, and in return He did not give me the keys to my dream. That is one of the reasons it took me longer to get into LDP. I was not releasing myself to be broken and rebuilt. I wanted to show God I was truly surrendering for this next season.

Right before I left for LDP, Lexie and I got baptized together at National Community Church in DC. She had recently dedicated her life to Christ, and I wanted to rededicate myself as one chapter closed and another opened. I knew there were more Goliaths ahead, and I knew I could only face them with God in control.
